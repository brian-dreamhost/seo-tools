Ensure Accessibility

A beautiful palette that fails WCAG 2.2 contrast requirements excludes visitors and creates legal risk. Check every text-and-background combination before deploying. If a color fails, get the nearest passing alternative that preserves your brand identity. Accessibility isn't an afterthought — it's a design constraint that makes everything better.

Design Tips for Small Businesses

1

Choose brand colors based on psychology, not preference

Blue conveys trust and is the most common color among top global brands. Red signals urgency and energy. Green suggests health and growth. Your personal favorite color might not be the right color for your business. Research shows that people form subconscious judgments about products within 90 seconds, and up to 90% of that assessment is based on color. Start with the emotion you want customers to feel, then find colors that evoke it.

2

Never skip contrast checking — it's a legal requirement

WCAG 2.2 Level AA requires a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text (18px+ bold or 24px+ regular). This isn't optional decoration advice — the ADA applies to websites, and thousands of web accessibility lawsuits are filed in federal court every year. Test every color combination before it goes live.

3

Generate all favicon sizes, not just one

A single 16x16 favicon won't cut it. Modern browsers need 16x16 for tabs, 32x32 for bookmarks, 180x180 for Apple devices, and 512x512 for PWA splash screens. Missing sizes mean your brand icon appears blurry or falls back to a generic placeholder on certain devices. The web.dev PWA guidelines specify 192px and 512px as minimum requirements.

4

Pair fonts by contrast, not similarity

The most reliable font pairing rule: combine a serif with a sans-serif. Using two sans-serif fonts that look nearly identical creates visual noise — it reads like an inconsistency rather than a design decision. Match fonts with similar x-heights so they feel harmonious at the same size. A standard rule of thumb in web typography is to limit your site to two typefaces — one for headings, one for body. Google Fonts' pairing guide covers the principles in detail.

5

Document your colors — even if your "brand guide" is a sticky note

Brand inconsistency happens when team members guess at colors instead of referencing exact values. At minimum, write down your primary, secondary, and neutral color hex codes and share them with anyone who touches your brand. A one-page color reference prevents the slow drift where your website is #0073EC but your email headers are #0066D4.

6

Extract colors from competitors to understand your market's visual language

Before picking your palette, analyze what your competitors use. If every competitor in your space uses blue and white, matching that palette makes you blend in — but deviating too far can feel out-of-category. The sweet spot is understanding the visual conventions of your industry and then making a deliberate choice about whether to conform or contrast. A brand color extractor can pull the exact hex values from competitor logos and websites, taking the guesswork out of competitive analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are these design tools really free?
Yes — all tools will be completely free with no account required when they launch. Every tool runs in your browser, so there's nothing to install and no usage limits. DreamHost is building these to help small businesses and creators build professional-looking brands without expensive design software.
How many colors should a brand palette have?
Most effective brand palettes use 3 to 5 colors: one primary color that carries your identity, one or two secondary colors for variety, and one or two neutral colors for backgrounds and text. Research from the Institute for Color Research shows that people make subconscious judgments about a product within 90 seconds — and up to 90% of that assessment is based on color alone.
What is WCAG contrast and why does it matter?
WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) defines minimum contrast ratios between text and background colors so that people with visual impairments can read your content. Level AA requires a 4.5:1 ratio for normal text and 3:1 for large text. Level AAA requires 7:1 and 4.5:1 respectively. Failing contrast means some visitors literally cannot read your website — and it can expose your business to ADA compliance issues.
What size should a favicon be?
You need multiple sizes: 16x16px for browser tabs, 32x32px for taskbars and bookmarks, 180x180px for Apple touch icons, and 192x192px plus 512x512px for Android and PWA manifests. A single favicon file is not enough — modern browsers and devices request different sizes for different contexts. Start with a high-resolution source image (at least 512x512) and a favicon generator can create all the required sizes from it.
How do I choose fonts that pair well together?
The safest approach is to pair a serif heading font with a sans-serif body font (or vice versa) — contrast in style creates visual hierarchy. Avoid pairing two fonts that are too similar, which looks like a mistake rather than a choice. Look for fonts with similar x-heights and proportions. Google Fonts offers over 1,500 free options, and a font pairing tool can show curated combinations with live preview so you can evaluate how they look together before committing.
What is a brand color extractor?
A brand color extractor analyzes an image — typically a logo or brand asset — and identifies the dominant and secondary colors used. It outputs the exact hex, RGB, and HSL values so you can replicate those colors consistently across your website, social media, print materials, and email templates. It's especially useful when you have a logo but no formal brand style guide, or when onboarding a new client and need to match their existing brand identity.
