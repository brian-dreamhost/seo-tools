Advertising Tips for Small Businesses

1

Lead with the benefit, not the feature

"Save 3 hours every week on invoicing" outperforms "Cloud-based invoicing software" every time. According to Google's ad copy best practices, ads that focus on what the user gains — rather than what the product does — see higher click-through rates. State the outcome first, then explain how you deliver it.

2

Know your break-even ROAS before you launch

If your average order value is $80 and your profit margin is 40%, you need a ROAS of at least 2.5:1 just to break even. The formula: break-even ROAS = 1 / profit margin, so 1 / 0.40 = 2.5. Most advertisers set arbitrary targets like "4x ROAS" without checking the math. Calculate your actual break-even point first, then set your target ROAS 20–30% above it to account for variance in performance.

3

Match your landing page to your ad — exactly

If your ad says "Free 14-day trial," the landing page headline should say "Start your free 14-day trial" — not "Welcome to our platform." Google uses landing page experience as a Quality Score factor, which directly affects your cost per click. Message mismatch doesn't just lose conversions — it makes your ads more expensive.

4

Design for the five sizes that matter most

Google's Display Network supports dozens of ad sizes, but five cover the vast majority of inventory: 300x250, 336x280, 728x90, 300x600, and 320x100. According to Google's responsive display ad guidelines, providing assets in these dimensions maximizes your reach without drowning your design team in spec sheets.

5

Improve Quality Score to pay less per click

Google's Quality Score is built on three factors: expected click-through rate, ad relevance, and landing page experience. A Quality Score of 7+ means you pay below-average CPCs. A score of 4 or below means you're paying a premium for every click. Fix your weakest component first — that's where the biggest cost savings are.

6

Start with $10/day, then scale what works

The biggest budget mistake is spending too much too early. Start with $10–20/day on 2–3 tightly themed ad groups. Run them for at least two weeks to get statistically meaningful data. Then double down on the campaigns with the best cost-per-acquisition and pause everything else. Google recommends setting budgets based on performance data, not guesswork.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are these advertising tools free?
Yes — completely free with no account required. Once launched, every tool will run in your browser with nothing to install and no usage limits. DreamHost is building these so small businesses can plan and optimize ad campaigns without paying for expensive advertising management platforms before they've even spent their first dollar on ads.
What is ROAS and how do I calculate it?
ROAS stands for Return on Ad Spend. It measures how much revenue you earn for every dollar you spend on advertising. The formula is simple: ROAS = Revenue from Ads / Cost of Ads. A ROAS of 4:1 means you earn $4 for every $1 spent. Most e-commerce businesses aim for a 4:1 ROAS minimum, though benchmarks vary by industry — service businesses often see higher ROAS while low-margin retail may need 8:1 or more to be profitable.
What size should my Google Display ads be?
Google recommends five core display ad sizes that cover most inventory: 300x250 (medium rectangle), 336x280 (large rectangle), 728x90 (leaderboard), 300x600 (half page), and 320x100 (large mobile banner). If you can only create a few sizes, start with 300x250 and 728x90 — together they cover the majority of available ad placements across the Google Display Network.
How do I write effective ad copy?
Effective ad copy follows a clear structure: lead with the benefit (not the feature), include a specific number or proof point, address the reader's objection, and end with a clear call to action. For Google Ads specifically, include your target keyword in at least one headline, use all available headline and description slots, and test multiple variations — Google's own data shows that advertisers who use all 15 headline slots in responsive search ads see 15% more clicks on average.
What makes a good landing page?
A good landing page matches the promise of your ad, loads in under 3 seconds, has a single clear call to action, and removes navigation that could distract visitors. The most critical elements are message match (the headline should echo the ad that brought them there), social proof (testimonials, review counts, trust badges), and a form or button above the fold. According to Google, 53% of mobile visitors leave a page that takes longer than 3 seconds to load — so speed is not optional.
How much should I spend on ads?
There's no universal answer, but a good starting point for small businesses is 5–10% of revenue on marketing, with paid ads making up a portion of that. Start small — $10–20/day on Google Ads is enough to test keywords and learn what converts. The key is not the total budget but the cost per acquisition: if a customer is worth $500 to your business and you can acquire them for $50 in ad spend, scaling up is profitable regardless of the total budget.
