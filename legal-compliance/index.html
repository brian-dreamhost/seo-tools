You process payments, collect shipping addresses, set cookies for cart persistence, and probably retarget visitors with ads. That's at least four privacy regulations intersecting at once. Legal & Compliance Tips for Website Owners

1

Your privacy policy must match your actual data practices

A generic privacy policy that mentions data you don't collect — or omits data you do collect — can create liability. If you use analytics tools, tracking pixels, session recording software, or any third-party service that processes visitor data, each one should be disclosed. The GDPR Enforcement Tracker shows that insufficient legal basis for data processing and lack of transparency are among the most common reasons for fines in the EU.

2

Cookie consent requires real choices, not just a notice

A banner that says "We use cookies" with only an "OK" button does not meet GDPR requirements. The European Data Protection Board requires that users can accept or reject non-essential cookies before they're set. That means analytics and marketing cookies must be off by default until the visitor actively opts in.

3

Accessibility failures are the fastest-growing source of website lawsuits

According to ADA Title III research, web accessibility lawsuits have risen significantly since 2017, with thousands filed annually in federal court alone. The most commonly cited issues include missing alt text on images, insufficient color contrast, and forms without proper labels. WCAG 2.2 Level AA is the standard courts and regulators reference most often, making it the practical benchmark for compliance.

4

GDPR can apply to your website even if your business is outside the EU

This is the part most U.S. business owners miss. GDPR's territorial scope extends beyond the EU — if you offer goods or services to people in the EU or monitor their behavior, GDPR applies regardless of where your business is located. Similarly, CCPA/CPRA applies if you meet certain thresholds and process data of California residents. Understanding which regulations affect you based on your audience is the first step toward compliance.

5

Terms of service protect you from disputes you haven't imagined yet

Without terms of service, you have no agreed-upon rules for user behavior, no limitation of liability, and no defined jurisdiction for disputes. If a user posts harmful content on your site, copies your work, or files a chargeback, your terms of service are your first line of defense. According to Nolo's legal guidance, enforceable ToS require clear acceptance mechanisms — not just a footer link.

6

Return policies must be visible before purchase, not just after

While the FTC's Mail, Internet, or Telephone Order Rule governs shipping timelines, many state laws require that return and refund terms be clearly disclosed before a transaction. Hiding a return policy in a footer that customers never see during checkout is a common mistake. A clear, accessible return policy also reduces chargebacks — card networks factor refund policy visibility into dispute outcomes, and clearly stated terms give you stronger footing when contesting disputes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are these legal tools free?
Yes — all tools will be completely free with no account required when they launch. Every tool will run in your browser, so there will be nothing to install and no usage limits. DreamHost is building these to help website owners handle basic legal compliance without paying for expensive compliance platforms. Important: these tools will generate templates and starting points, not legal advice. If you have complex legal needs or operate in a heavily regulated industry, you should have an attorney review the output.
Do I need a privacy policy?
If your website collects any personal data — including email addresses, cookies, analytics, or contact form submissions — you are legally required to have a privacy policy in most jurisdictions. GDPR (EU), CCPA/CPRA (California), PIPEDA (Canada), and many other laws mandate it. Even if you only use Google Analytics, you need one. A proper privacy policy should be customized to reflect what data you actually collect and which regulations apply to your audience.
What is GDPR and does it apply to me?
GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) is an EU law that protects personal data. It applies if you offer goods or services to people in the EU or monitor their behavior — regardless of where your business is located. If someone in Germany visits your website and you process their data via analytics, cookies, or forms, GDPR likely applies. Penalties for non-compliance can reach 4% of annual global revenue or 20 million euros, whichever is higher. Understanding your obligations starts with mapping what data you collect and where your visitors are located.
How do I make my website accessible?
Start with WCAG 2.2 Level AA — the most widely accepted standard. Key areas include: adding alt text to all meaningful images, ensuring keyboard navigation works on every page, maintaining a color contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text (3:1 for large text), labeling all form fields, and using proper heading hierarchy (H1, H2, H3 in order without skipping levels). Free browser extensions like axe DevTools or WAVE can help you identify issues, and testing with a screen reader reveals problems automated tools miss.
What should my cookie banner say?
Under GDPR, your cookie banner must clearly state what cookies you use and why, give users the option to accept or reject non-essential cookies before they are set, and link to your full cookie policy. A simple "We use cookies" banner with only an "OK" button does not meet GDPR requirements. You need granular consent controls — analytics and marketing cookies should each be separately opt-in, with only strictly necessary cookies active by default. The banner should also make it equally easy to reject cookies as to accept them (no dark patterns).
Do I need terms of service?
Terms of service aren't legally required in most jurisdictions, but they protect your business. Without them, you have no defined rules for user behavior, no limitation of liability, no intellectual property protections, and no dispute resolution process. If you sell anything, accept user-generated content, or run a SaaS product, terms of service are essential. An enforceable ToS should be customized to your business type, payment model, and jurisdiction — and should require clear user acceptance (not just a passive footer link).
